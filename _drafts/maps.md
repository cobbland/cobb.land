Following up on [The Purpose and Size of a Hex](/posts/purpose-and-size-of-a-hex/), building upon [The purpose of a hex and the best hex size (also encumbrance and speed considerations)](https://forum.illuminatedsnail.com/t/the-purpose-of-a-hex-and-the-best-hex-size-also-encumbrance-and-speed-considerations/47), and as part of the [Bandwagon: The Map is Not the Territory But It is the Topic](https://www.prismaticwasteland.com/blog/bandwagon-the-map-is-not-the-territory-but-it-is-the-topic), I present my thoughts on hex map traversal, a small hex map, and an adventure site.

---
Man, you do make some good points. I just went back to look at your campaign doc, and your wilderness travel rules do make sense. (Though I couldn't find rules on other movement speeds and encumbrance—says, "see sheet for details". What sheet, @Crow?!?!?)

You've very nearly convinced me that armor should have no (or little enough as to not be worth tracking) impact on movement speed. I've gone back and consulted several rule systems on how they handle speed and encumbrance. While Original and B/X (and directly inspired games) are certainly too granular, the newer old school (NSR?) does indeed seem to just hand-wave away encumbrance interacting with speed at all.

But I can't shake my experience backpacking and hiking. I can pretty easily hike twenty miles in a day when encumbered with no more than a meal and a jacket in a light backpack, but I'm hard-pressed to cover twelve when carrying food and supplies for a week outdoors. Disregarding armor, being fully equipped is tiring! Again, whether or not that is a compelling and meaningful thing to track in a game is up for debate, but I do think it could be!

As I am currently considering the 3-mile abstract hex, I'm toying with the following:

- ***Three levels of encumbrance:***
  - **Light.** Unequipped or only lightly equipped
  - **Normal.** Fully Equipped^[weapons and armor to fulfill the roll of your class, standard adventuring gear, rations for a week] (or unequipped while carrying treasure)
  - **Heavy.** Equipped and carrying treasure (or doubly equipped)
- ***Three overland "speeds":***
  - **Fast.** 2 hexes per watch^[my watches last four hours]—lightly equipped
  - **Normal.** 1 hexes per watch—normally equipped
  - **Slow.** 1 hex per 2 watches—heavily equipped
- ***Other considerations:***
  - **Weather and terrain.** May impose a -1 to -3 hex penalty per watch
  - **Haste.** Move +1 hex per watch, but there are other penalties
  - **Road, trail, or landmark.** Cannot get lost

I think the above works for a few reasons. One, with the added granularity of the 3-mile hex, mile considerations in speed can be done away with, skipping a step of calculations and speeding play. Two, fewer but more meaningful choices—"Do we ditch our equipment or our treasure? Do we pack extra gear and rations for unexpected eventualities or risk it and travel normal?" Three, I'm sure there's a third reason I can't think of.

I'm willing to do away with speed and encumbrance considerations in dungeons and encounters (my own games Fell and Peasant abstract them anyway), but I do feel they're important in overland travel. Agree to disagree, haha.

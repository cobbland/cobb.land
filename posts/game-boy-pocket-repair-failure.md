I wasn't even shopping for a Game Boy Pocket. I was looking for a good deal on a Game Boy Camera. The best price I saw at the time was an auction for a bundle that included the Camera and a pair of Pockets—red and blue—plus a Game Link Cable, GameShark, and Pelican Flip-n-Light. It started with a modest $35 bid, but by evening I was $92 in and the proud owner of way more Game Boy stuff than I had planned on getting. But it was all okay because I could fix them up, sell one to recoup my costs, and keep one to use with the Camera.

According to the listing, the Game Boys needed new screens—the original ones being "sun burned"—but worked otherwise. So I did some research and learned that the fix was just a "simple" matter of removing the top layer of polarizing film, scraping off some adhesive, and replacing the film with a sheet of after-market polarizer. The only downside to the operation being the newly refurbished screens would have a greenish hue more like the original Game Boy. Which was fine with me, because I planned on getting a DMG-style shell for the one I was going to keep anyway.

Should be easy!

Well, when the package arrived, neither of the Game Boy Pockets would turn on. With a little fiddling, they eventually both did, but only one actually showed the logo screen and chimed—the red one.

I had my work cut out for me, but I was undeterred.

<img class="gb" src="/images/IMG_PC02.png">

And here was where I made my first mistake. Instead of taking one apart and working on it until I either fixed it or broke it beyond repair, I opened them both.

Things went okay at first. The Boys disassembled, I soaked the shells in some mildly soapy water and cleaned the boards with q-tips and isopropyl alcohol. Red's board wasn't bad. Blue's, on the other hand, was covered in battery acid and corrosion. The PCB was bulging with the stuff.

There was little hope for Blue. It would have been a perfect time for me to set Red aside and use Blue as a learning experience—try all of my planned repairs when there was nothing to lose.

I didn't do that, though. I continued working on both at the same time.

On to the screen repair. I read some tutorials and watched several videos. They all said to scrub, scrub, scrub. Do you know what none of them said? Not once did one mention that one should be very careful during this operation, that the connection for the horizontal pixels is very fragile.

(Foreshadowing.)

Using the blade from a box cutter, q-tips, and about another twelve gallons of isopropyl alcohol, I went to town on those things. Peeling, scrubbing, wetting, waiting, and scrubbing some more. While I left one with its surface covered in alcohol, I scrubbed the other.

Back and forth I went over the course of two days. I probably spent six hours on those things. Until, finally, the screens were clean and free from sunburn and adhesive.

(As an aside, it turns out it isn't the polarizing film that get's burned—it's the adhesive: LOCA, or liquid optically-clear adhesive. In this case, I don't think it's _quite_ optically-clear, because it seems to be the adhesive that makes the Game Boy Pocket's screen gray instead of green.)

<img class="gb" src="/images/IMG_PC03.png">

With the Game Boys cleaned and reassembled, and with fresh polarizing film applied, the moment when all of my hard work would pay off had come.

Batteries in.

Power on.

Game Boy Pocket Blue: Nothing. No light. No screen. No sound. Completely dead.

Game Boy Pocket Red: Light and sound, but a screen crisscrossed with rows and columns of dead pixels.

Whatever had happened, surely there was a way to fix it. I'd followed all of the instructions to the letter (I thought).

Back to the internet, I finally managed to find all the videos of folks talking about how fragile the horizontal pixel connections are. And I even found a few tricks for how one might—just maybe if they're very, very lucky—fix dead lines of pixels: Through the gentle application of heat from a soldering iron in just the right spots, it is sometimes possible to reflow the underlying connections.

Vertical lines I managed to fix. But I handily killed even more of the horizontal lines of pixels, and I melted the connections a little bit while I was at it.

I'd failed. But I'd also learned a valuable lesson: If you have to perform a new delicate task multiple times, you should do each sequentially instead of all at once.

Had I worked on broken Blue first all the way to completion, I would have learned what to do and what not to do, and I probably would have been successful on my subsequent work on Red. Instead, I was left with one fully broken Game Boy, one that needed a replacement screen, and none that I could keep and play.

Well, that just wouldn't do. I'd gotten it in my head that I wanted a Game Boy Pocket (to go along with the too many handhelds I already had). So I devised a clever plan: Order more junky Game Boy Pockets, fix most of them up with new shells and screens, and sell them for a profit, using said profit to cover the cost of keeping one for myself—one with a fully functional original screen.

Now, I'm in for a few hundred dollars worth of (hopefully mostly functional) Game Boys and replacement parts. Fingers crossed.
